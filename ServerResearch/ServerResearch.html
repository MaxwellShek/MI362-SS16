            Chirs Piro, a Facebook software engineer from 2007 to 2011, writes on Quara.com that “The frontend is written in PHP (programming language). Much of it is written using XHP, and the runtime is HipHop for PHP. Like most websites there's a lot of JavaScript (programming language) running in the users' browsers” (Piro, 2010). He goes on to discuss the backend of Facebook services, which he states are made up of C, C++, Objective C, Python, Java and Erling, which the chat is written in. Piro also notes that much of the C++ services rely on boost (C++ Library), while the backend and frontend communicate through Apache thrift (a framework for developing languages in multiple languages).

            Since Facebook’s launch, open source has been a huge part of their developing philosophy. According to the current head of open course software at Facebook, “Nowadays, we use, maintain, and contribute to a significant number of major projects - in areas as diverse as native mobile tools, big data systems, client-side web libraries, backend runtimes and infrastructure, and, through the Open Compute Project, server and storage hardware” (Pearce, 2013). Pearce also discusses Facebook’s use of GitHub, where their account had over 90 repos, 40,000 commits and 15,000 forks as of 2013. Some specific open source projects with Facebook include collaboration with bountysoruce in an effort to motivate and reward work down in open source software; Facebook open academy, which aims to bring open source computer science programs to universities; and code.facebook.com, an engineering website keeping the public updated with their most recent activities (Pearce, 2013).

            Facebook does use open source control software and used GitHub until around 2014. They noted that due to extreme growth, they would be forced to redesign their system. Although Facebook intended to continue to use Github due to their comfort and familiarity with it, they decided Git’s internal working would be tough to scale to the point they wanted. Therefore, they looked to other places and decided to improve Mercurial, which is a distributed source control system mostly written in modular Python. Facebook noted problems with the speed of Mercurial compared to the speed of Git’s upon making the change. To combat this problem, they have contributed over 500 patches to increase the speed of Mercurial (Goode & Agarwal, 2014).

            Facebook does use a CDN, or content delivery network. The CDN they use is Akamai, which, according to many sources, is a global leader in the industry. This can be seen by viewing the source of the page on a browser such as Google Chrome. The .ak generally points to an Akamai server (Pipiat, 2012).

            Given the trillion monthly views of Facebook, the site needs a very large infrastructure in order to ensure proper functionality. Facebook’s infrastructure must be able to store all profile data such as photos and connected websites and applications. In 2010, Facebook announced their plans to build their own data centers, beginning in Prineville, Oregon. Since then, the company has built additional data centers across the United States and the world. According to DataCenterKnowledge.com, “this typically requires a larger up-front investment in construction and equipment, but allows greater customization of power and cooling infrastructure” (Data Center Knowledge, n.d.). Their customized center also allowed for more efficient energy use. The center uses less energy to do the same amount of work as their previous center.

            As stated in the previous paragraph, Facebook realized a need to build separate customized centers as the company grew due to the amount of data that needed to be stored. Building a customized center would allow Facebook to have a server that could handle would-be problems associated with the growth of data. They also publish how everything works in their warehouse, allowing for input to improve their practices for the most efficient server. Efficiency will be key for a server to store data and communicate with site users through servers as Facebook acquires more and more data from its users (Heiliger, 2011).

             In conclusion, Facebook uses various technologies for the functionality of its websites and servers. As the company has grown, so has the number of problems specifically associated with its server. Facebook has adapted to increased traffic and data through various solutions, such as building its own data centers. Learning about how and why Facebook and its server grew the way it did was very interesting.
